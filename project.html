Why do we see this Correlation?

The chart above shows a strong positive relationship between GDP and median income. (the size of the circles allow you to identify more populated countries)

In theory, wealthier countries have less individuals struggling financially. With increased wealth, there's generally an increase in the ability to engage in hobbys and spend less hours in formal work, resulting in increased life satisfaction. It is important to note that ineqeuality in a country interrupts the relationship between GDP per capita and happiness. If a countries' wealth is held by only a small percentage of the population, the opportunities previously mentioned are only accessible to a minority.

Does this relationship hold on a country level over time?

Event study on Finnish data (2013-2021)

The above chart depicts growth in Finnish GDP and happiness between the years 2013 and 2021. Finland's economy relies substantially on trade, and with large growth in exports in the years after 2015, GDP began to rise noticeably. In 2019, as the pandemic began, Finlands economy predictably shrank. It appears as though there is a delayed relationship between GDP and happiness. This seems logical as there will be a time delay between expansion/contractions in an economy and the response of business in lowering real wage or reducing size of workforce.

Bhutan, Costa Rica and Health

Bhutan is a small nation in the Himalayas that has neither a large GDP or great democratic quality, yet it consistently rank as one of the happiest countries in the world. Bhutan is a Buddhist country that focuses on Gross National Happiness rather than GDP. The people of Bhutan live a peaceful and spiritual life, free of materialism and social pressures.

Costa rica is another small nation that consistently ranks higher in the happiness rankings than its wealthier competitors. After abolishing its army in 1948, The Costa Rican government heavily fund the education sector and its national health system.

Though both nations differ in a multitude of ways, one thing they have in common is the tendency of their civilians to live a healthy lifestyle. I have used life expectancy to as an index to a healthy lifestyle and regressed happiness on this index in the graph below.

Conclusions

Having evaluated a few of the many contributors to happiness, I would make the conclusion that a balance of all mentioned and non-mentioned factors would be necessary for a happy society. A country with extremely high GDP but without Democratic quality or equality will be largely unhappy. Likewise, a healthy country with a life expectancy of 100 years will be unhappy if the individuals within it are unable to make their own decisions and lack civil liberty. On an individual basis, happiness is often a product of relationships and a sense of community. Using the evidence presented in this project, I would argue that the happiest countries are those that provide the infrastructure and foundations to allow communities and healthy social relationships to succeed.


Write Up

Aims of My Project:

My aim in this project was to give the reader a brief insight into what makes a country happier. The general opinion of the public would likely be that GDP per capita is the sole measurement that would produce a happier country, however I wanted to show that this alone not only not sufficient, but also not necessary at times (with the case study of Bhutan and Costa Rica). From a data science perspective, my aim was to use a mix basic charts, interactive charts, advanced charts and regressions, using both scraped and downloaded data to create a website that’s visually appealing. Another aim of mine was to make the page engaging, rather than a simple read. To accomplish this, I included the interactive single bar chart. Although this chart isnt the most effective way to portray the data, the fact that the user has to manually flick through the different countries hopefully results in a better engagement with the page.

Problems I encountered and how I overcame them:

The map of GDP/capita used data scraped from Wikipedia which was unclean in ways I had not previously encountered (for example there were master and sub column titles). I used colab to clean and export the data. I had to implement new code to remove leftmost character from country names (*) and also encountered distortion problems when exporting as normal csv, so exported as utf-8. Another Challenge I had in obtaining data was scraping the Civil liberty data. Pandas read_html function returned 'forbidden' when trying to scrape the website. As such, I had to use BeautifulSoup4 to ‘pretend to be a human’ to not have the scrape be blocked. To merge this data with the happiness data I imported both datasets into stata as I’m comfortable with merging data in this programme. I also had issues importing saved images into my visual studio code. This meant that once I had run a regression in colab. I had to reproduce the regression line with ‘fake data’ and layer it over the scatter plot in vega to portray the results of the colab regression.
